Secure your receivables in New Jersey. Understand the differences between residential and commercial filings, the "Notice of Unpaid Balance," and strict lodging deadlines.

⚠️ Critical NJ Requirement: Residential Arbitration

For residential construction contracts in NJ, you CANNOT just file a lien. You must first file a "Notice of Unpaid Balance" (NUB) and demand arbitration within 60 days of last furnishing. Only after an arbitrator approves can you file the actual lien. This process is strict and time-sensitive.

Key Deadlines for New Jersey Suppliers

Deadline Type Timing Details
Lodge Lien (Commercial) Within 90 days of last furnishing For commercial projects, the lien claim must be lodged for record with the County Clerk within 90 days of the last date of furnishing.
Notice of Unpaid Balance (Residential) Within 60 days of last furnishing Residential projects require this preliminary step (NUB) and arbitration before a lien can be filed.
Serve Copy of Lien Within 10 days of lodging You must serve a copy of the lien on the owner within 10 days of lodging it for record. (Also serve contractor/subcontractor).
Enforce Lien (Lawsuit) Within 1 year A lawsuit to enforce the lien must be commenced within 1 year of the date of the last provision of work/materials (Note: deadline runs from furnishing, not filing!).
Statutes NJ Construction Lien Law N.J.S.A. 2A:44A-1 et seq.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Skipping Residential Arbitration

Filing a lien directly on a residential project without the NUB and arbitration process is fatal to the lien and can result in liability for damages.

Missing the 90-Day Commercial Deadline

For commercial jobs, the 90-day window is strict. It counts from the last date of furnishing labor or materials, not the invoice date.

Failure to Serve within 10 Days

NJ requires service on the owner within 10 business days of lodging the lien. Failure to do so can invalidate the lien.

Confusing Public vs. Private

The Construction Lien Law applies to private projects. Public projects in NJ fall under the Municipal Mechanics' Lien Law or Bond Acts, which have totally different procedures.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between "lodging" and "filing"?

In NJ, you "lodge" the lien with the clerk. It is considered "filed" once the clerk accepts and records it. Deadlines typically hinge on the "lodging" date, but acceptance is key.

Do I need a written contract?

Yes. The NJ Construction Lien Law generally requires a written contract (signed by the party against whom the lien is asserted) or a writing evidencing the agreement.

Can suppliers to suppliers file a lien?

Generally, no. NJ law typically extends lien rights to those in direct contract with the owner, contractor, or a subcontractor. Remote suppliers (tier 3+) may not have rights.

What if the owner pays the contractor?

If the owner has paid the prime contractor in full before receiving a lien claim, the owner may have a defense (the "lien fund" concept). Filing early is crucial.

How long does a lien last?

A construction lien in NJ is valid for 1 year from the date of last furnishing. You must file suit to enforce it before that year expires.
