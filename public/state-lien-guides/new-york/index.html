New York is High Risk When "Last Furnishing" Is Mis-Traced

In New York, suppliers commonly lose lien rights because the last furnishing date is documented inconsistently (multiple deliveries, backorders, returns, pickup vs delivery, change orders). The lien deadline is measured in months, but the clock still starts from the last furnishing date that legally counts. Track deliveries and invoices tightly — then confirm your deadline.

Common Supplier Mistakes in New York

Using the Last Invoice Date Instead of the Last Furnishing Date

Invoices are accounting artifacts. Lien timing is driven by furnishing. If deliveries continued after the last invoice, or invoices continued after the last delivery, the wrong date can put you outside the filing window.

Assuming "8 Months" Means There's No Urgency

Collections often escalates late. If credit terms are 30–60 days and the account stalls, the project can drift past the last furnishing date quickly. Calendar the 8-month deadline as soon as furnishing ends.

Not Tracking Retainage-Driven Deadlines

Retainage timing can create an additional clock tied to when retainage becomes due. If your project uses retainage and payment timing is contractual, confirm when retainage is due and track the 90-day window.

Skipping Proof of Service

Serving required parties and keeping proof matters. If service is missing or unprovable, enforcement becomes harder even if the lien was filed on time.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is the New York lien filing deadline for suppliers?

This guide captures an 8-month filing deadline measured from the last furnishing date. The safest workflow is to log the last delivery that legally counts as furnishing, then calculate from that date.

Does New York require a preliminary notice for suppliers?

A single statewide supplier notice requirement is not captured in these raw facts. Because requirements can depend on project type and role, verify your path using the calculator and confirm with counsel when the claim is large.

Who needs to be served?

This guide captures service on the owner and prime contractor. Keep proof of service and confirm the exact service list for your project type to avoid enforcement issues later.

How does retainage affect timing?

If retainage is part of the claim, timing can be tied to when retainage payment becomes due. This guide captures a 90-day window after retainage is due. The key is knowing the contract-based retainage due date.

When do I have to enforce the lien?

This guide captures a 1-year enforcement period after filing the notice of lien. If the matter is not resolved, enforcement requires a timely lawsuit — filing alone does not secure payment.
